 Homeland security officials on Wednesday abruptly shelved a proposal to build a national database of license-plate scans after criticism from privacy advocates But since the solicitation was posted and featured in the Washington Post, privacy advocates have warned that the database sounded like a dragnet that would track the whereabouts of all drivers, including people who have done nothing wrong, and that the records might be held indefinitely But in a statement late Wednesday, the department announced a reversal  The solicitation, which was posted without the awareness of ICE leadership, has been canceled, said spokeswoman Gillian Christensen  The database should track vehicle license plate numbers that pass through cameras or are voluntarily entered into the system from a variety of sources (access control systems, asset recovery specialists, etc and uploaded to share with law enforcement, the original solicitation read The proposed National License Plate Recognition database was to have been used by immigration officers to find and arrest fugitives Supporters of license-plate scanning, like former New York state homeland security chief Michael Balboni, said it could have been an invaluable tool for finding dangerous suspects
